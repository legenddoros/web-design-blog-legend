Response

US Patent 6506148 B2, 2001

When I think of internet surveillance, one of the first things that comes to mind is US Patent 6506148 B2, 2001, titled "Nervous system manipulation by electromagnetic fields from monitors," which describes a method to theoretically influence a person's nervous system through weak, pulsed electromagnetic fields emitted by computer monitors and televisions. The patent doesn’t mean that the thing is actualized, and it deals with outdated CRT monitors, but it still raises concerns about surveillance. The ability for direct surveillance through our phones by the government seems plausible.

Aaron Swartz

Aaron Swartz was a brilliant programmer and digital rights activist who fought for a free and open internet. He briefly attended Stanford in 2004 but dropped out. He co-created RSS in 2000 at age 14, helped build Reddit in 2005, and believed knowledge should be accessible to everyone. After attempting to make academic research journals more freely available by downloading papers from MIT’s network, he faced severe federal charges under the Computer Fraud and Abuse Act. In 2013, while under prosecution, Swartz tragically ended his own life. This sparked widespread debate about the justice system, digital rights, and the ethics of aggressively prosecuting those who challenge control of information.

Alexandra Elbakyan

Another fascinating figure is Alexandra Elbakyan, a computer scientist and neuroscientist who founded Sci‑Hub (sci-hub.se) in 2011 to provide free access to millions of academic papers normally behind paywalls. She was sued in U.S. court for copyright infringement for $15 million in damages. She did not appear in court and lives outside U.S. jurisdiction, effectively operating Sci‑Hub from hiding. Sci-Hub’s current site is here.

Notable Court Cases

Carpenter v. United States (2018)

Accessing cell phone data requires a search warrant.

HiQ Labs v. LinkedIn (ongoing)

HiQ scraped public LinkedIn profiles; LinkedIn tried to block it. Courts recognized that public data may be accessible, but this case raises questions about ownership, consent, and automated data collection. It sets precedent for limits on data scraping versus corporate control of digital information.

The Age of Surveillance Capitalism by Shoshana Zuboff

I looked at my Instagram and Facebook ad history, and these line up with my search results. Cultural marketing differs from exclusionary marketing. If ad targeting excludes protected groups from opportunity, that’s illegal discrimination. Research shows housing and job ads can be delivered unevenly, sometimes skewing by gender. The biggest concern is how ads can shape opinions; Zuboff refers to this as “prediction products.”

In Summary

Don't be a mindless robot!

We must take responsibility for our own relationship with digital media. There needs to be strong advocacy, awareness, and legal protection from abuse by digital media platforms. This is ongoing as the landscape evolves.
