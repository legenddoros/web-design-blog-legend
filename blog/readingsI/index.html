The Geopolitics of information and connection

How global political ties influence the network system and international communication is worth noting. North Koreas fiber optic cables come from China and Russia with global internet only allowed to a select elite and researchers, a few thousand people. Most citizens use only the domestic intranet (Kwangmyong).

The lack of infrastructure connected to state limitations for citizens seems to have a snowball effect. Eritrea is often cited as having the least internet access, including no mobile intenet access for most citizens. Long-standing U.S. sanctions against Cuba prevented telecom investment and undersea cable access there for decades. Similiarly in Iran, sanctions have restricted access to Western telecom equipment and finance.Being geographicaly landlocked aids the goverment of Turkmenistan's restriction of internet to its citizens.

Conversely, the countries with the fastest internet speeds are, loosely in order, Singapore, United Arab Emirates, Hong Kong, France, Iceland, Denmark, Netherlands, Switzerland, United States, South Korea, and honorable mentions, Sweden, Norway, Japan, and Taiwan. While considered the best intelligence agneices are United States, United Kingdom, China, Russia, and Israel. Paints an interesting global picture.

Undersea cables carry ~95%+ of global data traffic, and are vulnerable to interception and survellience. Large tech companies (Google, Amazon) now fund or own major undersea cables, shifting influence to private platforms.

Underwater Cables and the environment

Cable laying and burial can disturb natural ecosystems. In areas like the North Sea and the Mediterranean, cable burial disturbed benthic habitats by resuspending sediments that smother slow-growing corals and sponges, which recover very slowly.

Cables are designed to last 25 years but upgrades and repairs can vary due to friction, natural occurences and vulnerabilty in shallow placement.

In 2008, the Taiwan cable breaks, due to seabed earthquakes, damaged connections to Hong Kong, Japan, South Korea, and other parts of East Asia, causing widespread internet slowdowns and disruptions to financial and communication networks.

How to See Invisible Infrastructure

Ingrid Burrington, 2015

The 14th International Utility Locate Rodeo sounds like it would be an intersting thing to visit. I had no idea that "telecommunications firms are legally obligated to make their cables easy for excavators to find", although I have spotted them on the streets and on the sides of buildings, as I walk alot. I actualy find these symbols fascinating.

This rodeo seems like a small compensation for a trade that pays so little, given its importance, kind of like paramedics and EMTs.

It makes sense that alot of professional locators and certifications became popular after 2000 with the expansion and popularity of the internet. “No one really understands how much stuff is in the ground,” the writer said she was told repeatedly by locators. Not even locaters even know. Makes you wonder if some of these are hidden on purpose.

I came across the Time Warner Cable logo and never realized that its the combination of an eye and an ear. Also kind of looks like the Egyptian heiroglyph Eye of Horus - very ominous.

The end of the article poses the idea of "invisible" things versus our active decision as individuals to ignore things. This reminded me of Milan Kunderas idea in "The Art of the Novel", that describes society as a structure that sees things in black and white, and its our job (and the writers), to discover, to see the grey areas.
